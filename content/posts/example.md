In the tactical language of modern football, few terms are confused as often as "full-back" and "wing-back." They operate in the same areas of the pitchâ€”the wide channelsâ€”but their roles, responsibilities, and the systems they play in are fundamentally different.

Understanding the difference between a full-back and a wing-back is crucial to understanding a manager's tactical setup. Is the team built on solid defence, or is it designed to stretch the play and attack from the flanks?

This article will break down the key differences in a simple, easy-to-understand way, complete with diagrams.



## âš½ What is a Full-Back?

A full-back is a defender first and foremost. This role is used in a formation with four players in defence, such as a 4-4-2, 4-3-3, or 4-2-3-1.

The two defenders in the middle are "centre-backs," and the two defenders on the outside are the "full-backs" (a left-back and a right-back).



### Key Responsibilities:

* Defensive Duty: Their primary job is to defend the wide areas. This means stopping the opposing team's wingers, blocking crosses, and "tucking in" to support the centre-backs when the ball is on the other side of the pitch.
* Attacking Support: In the *modern* game, the "modern full-back" is also expected to support the attack. They do this by "overlapping" (running past) their own winger to provide an extra option for a pass or a cross.
* The Key Relationship: A full-back almost *always* has a winger or wide midfielder playing directly in front of them. They work together to control the flank.

> In short: A full-back is a defender in a four-person defence who supports the attack.

A wing-back is a hybrid roleâ€”part defender, part attacker. This role is *only* used in a formation with three centre-backs (which becomes a "back-five" when defending), such as a 3-5-2 or 3-4-3.

Because there are three central defenders, the wing-back is given more freedomâ€”and more responsibility.



### Key Responsibilities:



* Control the Entire Flank: This is the most important difference. A wing-back is often the *only* player designated to the wide channel. They are responsible for *both* attacking like a winger and defending like a full-back.
* Attacking Duty: When their team has the ball, the wing-back's primary job is to attack. They push high up the pitch to provide width, deliver crosses, and even get into the box to score. They are a primary attacking outlet.
* Defensive Duty: When the team loses possession, the wing-back must sprint back to join the three centre-backs, forming a solid five-player defensive line.
* High Stamina: The "what does a wing-back do" question can be answered with one word: *everything*. This role is arguably the most physically demanding on the pitch, requiring incredible stamina to run "box-to-box" for 90 minutes.

Notice the Wing-Back (WB) is positioned much higher and has no winger in front of them. They are responsible for the entire length of the sideline, joining the attack but racing back to defend.

> In short: A wing-back is a hybrid player in a five-person defence who is responsible for the *entire* flank.

### Is a Wing-Back More Attacking Than a Full-Back?



Yes, absolutely. A wing-back's starting position is higher, and their main role when the team has the ball is to create scoring chances. A full-back's main role is to stop them.



## Conclusion: It's All About the System



The difference between the roles isn't just a name; it's a fundamental tactical choice by the manager.

* A manager who chooses full-backs (in a back-four) often prioritises defensive solidity in the centre and uses traditional wingers to attack.
* A manager who chooses wing-backs (in a back-three/five) wants to stretch the opposition, attack aggressively from the flanks, and rely on the wing-backs to cover a huge amount of ground.

So, the next time you watch a match, look at the formation. Do you see a back-four or a back-three? That will give you the answer and tell you exactly what that player's job is.
